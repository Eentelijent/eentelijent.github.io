Summary

Learning Overview

In this topic, the following is taught:

  • Relationships between people and nature in their neighbourhoods

Key Points

  • Local communities and nearby nature areas are:
    • Dependent upon each other
    • Mutually affect each other
  • Nature brings benefits to people by:
    • Removing pollution
    • Lowering temperatures
    • Providing spaces for recreation
  • People bring benefits to nature by:
    • Causing soil erosion and damaging vegetation
    • Worsening pollution
    • Disturbing wildlife
  • Nature may bring disadvantages to people as:
    • Wildlife attacks can harm oeioke
    • Environmental pollution can limit development(s)

Content concepts

  • Community — A group of people in a particular place who have a sense of belonging both to the place and to their group.
  • Nature Area — The natural environment; refers to the non-human world, which are areas that are untouched/barely affected by humans.
  • Recreation: — Any voluntary activity undertaken for purposes of enjoyment, pleasure, and personal satisfaction.
  • Neighbourhood — A sub-division in an urban area that is distinguished by some character or quality (mental attatchment), where residents share commonalities.
  • Wildlife — Animals of a wild nature, including their young or eggs.
  • Vegetation — The collection of plant life that provides ground cover in a region.
  • Environmental protection — Any activity to protect, conserve abd restonre nature habitats and (their) species.
  • Soil erosion — Soil particles being detatched, transported and deposited by water, wind, gravity, animals, or plants.
  • Pollutant — Substances that are present in concentrations that may harm humans, plants and animals.
  • Pollution — The introduction of harmful substances into the environment.

What is the relationship between people and nature?

  1. Local communities and nearby nature areas are dependent upon each other.
    • People are dependent on nature for many things:
      • Nature provides essentials to living such as air and water
      • Living organisms like bees pollinate crops for a continuous supply of food
      • Nature provides spaces for recreation and leisurely activites
        • Allows people to bond with their family and friends

          • This allows humans to maintain their mental well-being
        • People can maintain their physical well-being
        Example:
        • NParks' Therapeutic Gardens which help to connect people with nature and reduce their stress
    • Some nature areas are also dependent on local communities to thrive.
      • People in the neighbourhood may tend to the health of nearby natural areas, ensuring that the plants and animals there thrive.
      • Individuals in local communities can encourage one another to enjoy nearby natural areas in a sustainable manner without causing irreversible damage.
        • Example:
        • NParks organises a range of activities for schools during the week of international day of bio-diversity in May, such as the Green Wave campaign which aims to educate youths about the importance of protecting our natural environment.

  2. Local communities and nearby nature areas mutually affect each other.
    • In a mutual relationship, the parties involved (communities and nature) can bring about benefits to each other.
    • Benefits to people: Green spaces and urban wildlife in neighbourhoods can enhance the mental health and well-being of local communities.
    • Benefits to nature areas: Human activites can enhance their biodiversity
      • People can gain enjoyment and enhance their mental health (same as above)
      • The overall health of the natural environment is enhanced as more shelter and habitat for urban wildlife to thrive are provided.

    How do people and nature bring about benefits to each other?

    1. Nature areas benefit people by lowering temperatures, removing pollutants and providing space for recreation.
      2. Lowering air temperature Removal of pollutants Provision of recreational space
      • The presence of vegetation
        • Provides shade, reduces the amount of direct sunlight reaching the surface
        • Cools the surrounding air through evapo-transpiration
          • Water that plants absorb through their roots is released into the air as water vapour
          • Evapo-transpiration uses heat from the surroundings, thus cooling the surroundings
      • During photosynthesis, vegetation absorbs carbon dioxide and other air pollutants, releasing them as oxygen.
      • Small particles (PM10 &c.) are intercepted by leaves, which are then washed to the ground as it rains.
      • Wetland plants can trap suspended materials and absorb excess nutrients in water, thus improving water quality.
      • Natural areas offer numerous opportunities for outdoor activities
      • Direct and frequent interactions with nature can bring about health and well-being benefits
        • This can reduce the severity and occurence of health conditions

    2. People bring about benefits to nature through community activites that promote the importance of environmental protection.
      • Local communities can collectively raise awareness about the value of nature areas through encouraging positive behaviour that does not damage nature.
        • Example:
        • The NParks Citizen Science initiative 《 Dragonfly Watch 》 trains participants to identify different dragonfly species at their assigned sites, resulting in greater protection of the species (pl.) via raising awareness of their importance in the environment
      • Local communities can also organise or participate in conservation efforts.
        • Clean-up programmes to remove pollutants can help restore natural habitats and provdie a healthy environment for species to thrive
          • Example:
          • The waterway clean-up programme, organised by the Waterways Watch Society (WWS) creates opportunities for schools to pick up rubbish from the Marina Reservoir.

    What are the disadvantages people and nature bring to each other?

    1. People cause soil erosion, damage vegetation, worsen pollution and disturb wildlife
      2. Causing soil erosion and damaging vegetation:
      • When people hike along non-designated trails in nature areas:
        • This may cause soil compaction which prevents rainwater from infiltrating the surface. This leads to high surface flows and soil erosion as flowing water washes away the soil particles.
        • They may trample on vegetation, damaging plants and affecting their growth.
        Example:
        • In 2014, human activites in Bukit Timah Nature Reserve caused such severe soil erosion and habitat degradation that public access to the nature reserved had to be limited for 2 years.

      Worsening pollution and disturbing wildlife:
      • Littering can worsen pollution in natural areas
      • Animals can get injured by sharp objects (e.g. metal cans) or become entangled with plastic containers and bags
      • Animals can mistake rubbish for food and consume them, causing them to get hurt and eventually lead to their death
      • Feeding wild animals may also lead to changes in the habits and behaviour of wildlife. Human-wildlife conflict can increase when animals associate food with people
        • Example:
        • Some people think that they are doing a favour by feeding wild animals, but this can cause them to become reliant on humans, doing the more harm than good. Anyone caught intentionally feeding wildlife is fined $5,000 on the first offence.

    2. Nature may bring disadvantages to people as wildlife from nearby natural areas may harm people and environmental protection may limit development
      • Human-wildlife conflicts may increase due to an increase in human-wildlife encounters *(like said above)

      Figure 2: Table showing causes that increase human-wildlife encounters in Singapore.

      • Singapore continues to urbanise and develop, leading the natural habitats shrinking. Wildlife may venture out of nature areas in search of food, potentially increasing human-wildlife encounters.
      • Growing interest in outdoor activities has led to people spending more time in natural areas, thus increasing the frequency of human-wildlife encounters.
      • Singapore aims to birng nature back to the city and promote human-wildlife encounters.

      • As the wildlife population in the city increases, people who may not know how to deal with them may unintentionally provoke them, leading to attacks.
      • Some animals may be confused and fearful when venturing into unfamilliar urban spaces, increasing their likelihood of aggitation and attacks.
      • Environmental protection may be perceived to be limiting development.
        • Some may feel that the protection of nature is done at the expense of their needs/wants.
          • Example:
          • The Cross Island MRT line was propsed to be built around and not under the Central Catchment Natural Reserve.
          • Residents feared that this would cause them:
            • Inconvenience (construction works and longer travelling times)
            • Damage their property (???)
            • Result in their homes getting acquired to make way for construction
          • However, the nature reserve is home to numerous critically endangered species. Construction of the Cross-Island MRT Line under the Reserve may damage their habitat, affecting their survival
