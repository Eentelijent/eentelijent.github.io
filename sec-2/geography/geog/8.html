I. Different types of Housing

1. Housing

  • housing refers to structures/buildings developed to shelter people from the elements
  • often seen as a basic need that everyone should have access to
  • housing can be built in a planned or unplanned fashion
Classification of housing

Housing can be classified according to height and density
  • high rise, high density: nearer to city centre
  • low rise, low density: around the fringe of a city

2. Types of Housing

Formal Housing Informal Housing
  • legally built housing
  • often provided by government housing programmes/ private developers
  • illegally-built housing
  • often built by people with no access to formal housing

3. Features of formal housing

  1. Built by government or private developers

    • in singapore, all public housing is developed by the HDB.
    • such public housing can only be sold to citizens or permanent residents
    • plots of land may be sold to private businesses, who in turn develop private housing projects (e.g. condos)

  2. Legal rights to occupy land

    • Definition: the ability of individuals to freely occupy, acquire (obtain), use and possess land at their own discretion, so long their activities do not impeded the individual rights of others

    • formal housing refers to housing that is built by people with legal right to occupy the land
    • these properties may then be leased or sold to others

  3. Access to basic services

    • services include:
      • provision of sanitation services
      • access to water and electricity
    • Infrastructure to support such services are also included:
      • underground water pipes
      • electrical sub-stations
      • power lines
      • sewage pipes (separate from water pipes)

    High-quality building materials

    • could includes materials such as:
      • concrete
      • metal
      • hardwood

    • such materials may vary depending on:
      • economic circumstances
      • environmental circumstances
      • social circumstances
    • In Singapore, all housing must conform to the specifications for building safety and quality drawn by the Building and Construction Authority (BCA).

4. Features of informal housing

  • Mainly found in the outskirts and underdeveloped areas of cities
  • Commonly known as squatter settlements
Commonly have issues such as
  • Social Unrest
  • Degradation of environmental quality
  • Widespread diseases

1. Self-built squatter settlements

  • Typically built by individuals in an unplanned version
  • Often constructed from scavenged materials
    • repurposed zinc sheets
    • recycled timber
  • Usage of inferior building materials → risk of:
    • Collapsing,
    • Catching fire easily,
    • Flooding on rainy days


2. No legal right to occupy land

  • Land is built out of necessity
  • Many informal houses occupy government land or private property which is undeveloped and unsecured
  • Residents are often illegal occupants of the land
  • Constant risk of losing their homes (eviction and houses cleared by developers/government)

3. Lack of access to basic services

  • Not built in areas not meant for housing development,
    • Lack of access to basic services (electricity, water and sanitation) --> residents resort to:
      • illegal tapping onto nearby electric grinds,
      • syphoning water from nearby pipes,
  • disposing sanitary waste into the ground or nearby rivers.
    • Such practices increase the risk of waterborne diseases, consumption of contaminated water, and ill-health due to electrocution

III: Factors affecting the Location of Housing
Land-Use Planning
Definition: guidelines drawn by planning authorities
Common type of guideline: Zoning

Zoning
Restricts the type of activities and land-use permitted on specific sites
Shapes layout of cities
Enables various types of development

Developers
It can be affected by whether it is developed by private or public developers
Private developers: chooses more commercially-viable sites
Government: chooses people’s needs over profits
*Informal housing tend to occupy locations near large housing or industrial developments

Land Prices
High land prices = houses have to be sold at higher prices
People will no longer be able to afford them
Governments must regulate land prices
High land prices may cause informal housing

Housing financial support
Developers
Lower the cost of building houses for developers
Can encourage developers to build more houses → prevent housing shortage
Otherwise, most people will not be able to afford formal housing
Allows buildings to be more affordable

People
Governments provide funding and subsidies to buy housing
BTO, HDB allow people to buy affordable formal housing
