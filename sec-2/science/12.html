Chapter 12

Interactions within ecosystems

General overview

  • Ecosystems — different populations in a community interacting with one another and their physical environment in a habitat
    • Micro-ecosystem — smaller areas within a large ecosystem
      • Community — different populations of plants, animals and other organisms living together in the same habitat
        • Population — all individuals of a given species living in a habitat
          • Species — a group of inter-breeding organisms that do not necessarily (but can) with members of other groups
      • Environment — everything around an organism that affects its way of life
        • Biotic — living things
        • Abiotic — physical things (e.g. physical environment
      • Habitat — natural home of an organism; where it lives and re-produces
        • pond
        • rock pools
        • trees
        • bodies of larger animals

Ecology

Definition: The study of interactions between living organisms and their environment

Important factors:

  • ample supply of food
  • shelter from predators
  • protection from bad weather
  • places for reproduction, hibernating and nesting

The physical (abiotic) environment

  • Climate is a very important factor of the physical environment
  • The physical environment affects the types of organisms that live in it
  • Organisms also have special characteristics (adaptations) that enable them to live successfully in their environment
  • It is made of non-living physical factors

Light

Features

  • All green plants require light energy to make food
  • All forms of life depend directly or indirectly on green plants for food
  • Light enables organisms to see
    • find for food
    • avoid dangers (predators, cliffs etc.)

Adaptations

  • Fireflies produce their own light — attract mates
  • Angle fish — lure prey
  • Bats — echolocation

Air

Features

  • Most organisms require oxygen to live
    • land organisms take in oxygen from the air (gaseous)
    • aquatic organisms take in oxygen that is dissolved in water

Adaptations

  • certain species of fish such as the guppy can live in water low in dissolved oxygen by gulping gaseous oxygen from the air

Temperature

Features

  • the amount of heat received from the sun —> temperature of the place
  • most organisms are active around 0º to 45º
  • organisms have a limited temperature range where they can carry out vital life processes
  • organisms have an optimum range where they are most active
    • humans: (if lesser: hypothermia) 35ºC to 40ºC (if above: life-threatening fever)

Outliers

  • some aquatic animals & plants can remain active at freezing temps
  • some invertebrates live in hot springs with temperatures ~500ºC
  • some algae can grow in waters of up to 80ºC

Adaptations

  • plants: deciduous plants shed leaves during winter —winter
  • animals: some animals hibernate — winter
  • some birds migrate
  • polar bears, seals and, penguins have a thick layer of blubber and fur to keep themselves warm

Water

Features

  • Water is essential for life
  • Water is present in the environment of every organism
  • Water is the medium of aquatic organisms
  • Tropical rain-forests have high water content — supports >50% of the world's biotic species

Adaptations

Cactus:

  • modified leaves (not 'spikes'!) — cut down on water loss via transpiration
  • modified stem — store water
Camel:
  • can drink lots of water at one time
  • hump(s) containing fats — can be broken down to give water & energy

Minerals

Features

  • Required by plants for healthy growth
  • Obtained from the soil
  • Nitrates (a mineral) is required for plants to make proteins
  • Fertilisers have minerals to sustain plant life
  • Minerals that animals need are obtained via diet or via dissolved minerals in water

Examples

  • calcium — milk
  • iron — green leafy vegetables

Acidity/Alkalinity

Features

  • Acidity/alkalinity of water determines the type of aquatic organisms that can live in it
  • Acid rain can kill trees

Examples

  • fresh-water organisms live in a water of around pH7 (neutral)
  • marine organisms live in waters of around pH8 (slightly alkaline)
  • most plants grow well in neutral/slightly alkaline soils
  • some plants such as pineapples (tharman?) and cotton grow better in acidic soil

Predator-prey relationship
  • A type of relationship within the biotic environment
  • Predators: hunt other animals for food
    • usually have strong jaws, sharp teeth and claws to hunt and kill prey
  • Prey: hunted animals

Adaptations

Camouflage
  • Adaptation that disguises an organism
  • Enables predators to creep up on its prey unseen

Examples

  • Chameleon — can change colour to match with surroundings
  • Stick insect
  • Leaf insect
  • Stone fish
  • Orchid mantis
Poison
  • protect from predators
  • used to kill prey

Examples

  • Millipedes and caterpillars — secretions that can cause blisters & itchiness
  • Venus fly trap — uses venom to lure and kill flies

Energy transfer


  • All energy comes from the sun
  • Energy flows within an ecosystem when organisms feed on each other

Decomposers

  • Decomposers —break down dead bodies of living things into simpler substances
    • Fungi
    • Bacteria
    • Scavenger
  • Scavengers — feed on dead plants and animals, break them down into smaller fragments
    • Vulture
    • Hyena
    • Crow
    • Earthworm
    • Ants
  • Fungi and Bacteria — break down dead organisms into simpler compounds

Features

  • such compounds are then released into the soil/air
  • these can be re-used over again by plants and animals
  • decomposers are important in the carbon and nitrogen cycles

Carbon Cycle

  • All living organisms contain carbon
  • Carbs, proteins and fats all contain carbon
  • Carbon is released through the air as carbon dioxide
  • The circulation of carbon is known as the carbon cycle.

CO2 is released by:

  • burning of fossil fuels
  • respiration
    • *** Decomposers do not directly release CO2 into the air

CO2 is used by:

  • plants (photosynthesis)
